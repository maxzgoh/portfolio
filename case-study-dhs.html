UI Case Study.

Department of Homeland Security.

The U.S. federal government department on a mission to enhancing the security of the country.

Scope.

With a plethora of information and topics handled by the DHS, I sought to improve the architecture, brand, and design of their site to allow increase usage, favorability, and ease-of-use for visitors. My main focus was the user interface, branding, navigation, and content strategy.

Brand Voice.

The objective was to refresh the interface brand by highlighting the voice of strength, security, and responsibility while introducing additional undertones of simplicity and modernity through redesigning the website. I wanted to provide clean, purposeful, and modern design to increase consideration, usage, and overall satisfaction for users of all age groups. I focused on the homepage as it is the first point that users visit.

Site Map.

I started to browse the website and I noticed that the content was a bit scattered. There were articles that were placed into topics that did not best describe the information. So I created a site map with clearer topics of information that would make it more intuitive and easier for users to navigate and find articles that they are looking for.

Red-lining.

I also redlined the homepage to understand the dimensions and layout behind the existing website for desktop, mobile, and tablet.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes.

After rearranging the information architecture and redlining the homepages, I drafted wireframes for all devices with the goal making information clearer and more accessible for all users.

Global Sticky Navigation: I introduced a global sticky navigation with helpful icons for users to begin looking for information at any point of the website.

Search Bar: Also, the search bar is front and center of the hero image so users can quickly type in key words or questions that they would like answered. This shortens the time spent clicking through all the pages to find the content that the user is looking for.

More Specific Naming of Topics: To provide further ease of use, I created new names for the topics and call-to-actions that would better represent the information.

User Testing.

Objectives.

I set up a user test plan after designing the initial wireframes and set up the following test objectives:

  • To determine design inconsistencies and usability problem areas within the navigation menu.
  • To exercise website under controlled test conditions with participants.
  • To establish baseline user performance and user-satisfaction levels of the user interface for future usability evaluations.
  • To identify potential design concerns to improve the efficiency, productivity, and end-user satisfaction.

Tasks.

Below are the instructions provided during the test.

  1. Find the "Join Frequent Travelers Program" page on current website.
  2. Provide the reason why they chose to use their chosen method to search for the news page
  3. Find the "Join Frequent Travelers Program" page on clickable prototype.
  4. Provide the reason why they chose to use their chosen method to search for the news page
  5. Give open opinion about the nagivation experience between the two websites.

Results.

  • 1/5 of testers immediately used the search bar to type in "Join Frequent Travelers Program" in the prototype.
  • 4/5 of testers were able to find the "Join Frequent Travelers Program" page without any detours on the current website versus 100% for the prototype.
  • 5/5 of testers preferred more specific navigation bar selections, such as "For Travelers", compared to the "How Do I?" (current website) call-to-action.
  • 5/5 of testers felt that the prototype was better structured than the current.
  • 5/5 of testers liked the drop down menu feature in the prototype because they were able to view their sections before clicking on a selection.

Based on the results, it appears that (1) the placement of the search bar became a stronger option for users, (2) specific topics in the global navigation bar were helpful for users to find what they are looking for, (3) the new layout and content structure was more understandable for users, (4) and drop down menus bettered their search experience.

Style Guide.

Once I completed the user testing and made adjustments to the wireframes based on the results, I then created a style guide to keep the interface elements consistent. The colors were inspired by the DHS emblem and we decided to use the navy blue as one of the primary colors to respresent patriotism and strength.

Conclusion.

The goals of the redesign tackled the issues around usability, branding, navigation, and information architecture. The interface was redesigned for users to more easily navigate through the pages. Moreover, the brand voice of strength, security, and responsibility were exemplified through the new style guide, reinforcing their identity while providing a clean and modern look. Lastly, the navigation and reorganization of the content ultimately helped users search for what they were looking for within the library of information that this government website holds.

As this was more of a user interface focused study, the next steps would be to tackle more user pain points during searching and figuring methods to reduce the number of clicks to find the content that the visitor is searching.
